Is H1 training on our reports?

When I saw the H1 Platform announcement, I got frustrated by how unclear HackerOne still is about whether researcher data can be used for AI training or AI-powered testing. The same questions had already been raised publicly a few months ago, but the public wording still felt vague to me. So I went back through the terms and conditions to see what changed over time, and what those changes allow compared to the public statements.

What follows is a non-exhaustive timeline of those changes. I have tried to stay factual, while also showing the strongest concern the wording could reasonably allow. This is not proof of what HackerOne has done; it is a reading of what the terms and statements leave open.

As this timeline is not the easiest to read through, here is a small recap of what I found:

  • There is a clear distinction between aggregated/anonymized data from reports and the report itself.
  • What that aggregated/anonymized data contains is not precisely defined anywhere I could find.
  • Intellectual property, which we keep for our reports, mainly concerns the exact phrasing of the report, not the knowledge, novel research, private tools, or techniques behind it, such as a non-public CVE proof of concept or anything similar.
  • Public statements and docs are more protective than the broadest reading of the terms, but they are not legal protection for researchers, and they are not the same as a binding commitment from HackerOne in the terms.

This is the line most often cited to reassure researchers: you keep your IP. But IP is not the whole question. Copyright mainly protects the text/expression of your report, not the underlying ideas, procedures, or methods in the same way.

Techniques, novel attack ideas, and know-how may be protected by other things in some situations, including contract, confidentiality, trade secret, patents, or program rules, but "you retain your IP" does not by itself tell you whether the substance of the research can be used for product improvement or training.

And as the grant on the left shows, the license you do give is perpetual, irrevocable, sublicensable, transferable and worldwide, limited by the stated purpose, which points to §3, the section that permitted AI training at the time. So you can keep ownership and still have granted broad use rights. Ownership here answers less than it first appears to.

The clause permitting use of Confidential Information to train AI models was removed. In the same revision, "training" now appears within the Non-Identifying Data permission, consented without restriction for "developing… future products or services."

Non-Identifying Data is defined as aggregated and anonymized information from reports and other platform/customer data. Anonymization removes identifying details, but the terms do not explain how much technical detail is removed. Binding contract term

Revised Community Member Terms, §8.2 & Platform Tools §2.2

Effective May 11, 2026

§8.2: "…a perpetual, irrevocable, non-exclusive, transferable, sublicensable, worldwide, royalty-free license to use, copy, reproduce, display, modify, adapt, transmit, and distribute copies of that Community Member Data for our business purposes, including to provide and develop our products or services."
Platform Tools §2.2: "…a perpetual, irrevocable, worldwide, fully paid-up and royalty-free license… to analyze, create derivative works from, and otherwise use PT Output generated by any Platform Tool… for… improving… all HackerOne products and services."

Context: §8.2 covers Community Member Data generally. Platform Tools §2.2 is separate and covers PT Output. "PT Output" (§1.2) is content a Platform Tool generates "by leveraging AI, machine learning and other automated technologies." HackerOne's May 2026 update lists the "Hai Report Assistant Agent" as an example of an assistive tool.

hackerone.com/terms/community hackerone.com (May 2026 update notice)

The prior version licensed your data "for providing and improving the Services" and tied that purpose back to §3 of the General Terms. This version changes the purpose to "for our business purposes, including to provide and develop our products or services," and the cross-reference that previously bounded it is gone.

That is a real broadening. "Business purposes" and "develop our products or services" are wider than simply providing and improving the existing Services. Combined with the current §3 language, it gives HackerOne much more room to use Community Member Data for product development, not just to operate the bug bounty workflow.

Then there is a separate Platform Tools clause for PT Output. That matters because HackerOne's May 2026 update points to AI assistive tools such as the Hai Report Assistant Agent. If a report, or part of a report, is generated with an H1-provided AI tool, that generated output may fall under PT Output.

The two grants are not the same. The normal reports license (§8.2) lets HackerOne use, copy, reproduce, display, modify, adapt, transmit and distribute Community Member Data. The PT Output license (§2.2) adds stronger verbs the reports license does not name: "analyze," "create derivative works from," and "otherwise use." So using an H1-provided AI tool can put at least the AI-generated part of the report under a broader grant than if you wrote it yourself.

My personal opinion

I don't think the trust boundary between a bug bounty platform, programs, and hackers should be based on public posts, interviews, or help-center pages. It should come from clear terms, written with precise wording that keeps every party protected.

As of now, I find HackerOne's terms clear on the protection of customer private and confidential data, meaning the programs. But they feel much more open when it comes to what the platform can do with hacker data. By hacker data, I don't only mean the report text itself. I mean the intelligence that can be extracted from it: the techniques, patterns, attack paths, and research ideas behind the report. In my opinion, there is still not much in the terms that clearly protects hackers' novel research and ideas.

I believe hackers will always find a way to find new vulnerabilities. But if platforms can extract and scale that knowledge before researchers choose to make it public, they may prevent those researchers from being able to monetize the work they invested in. In the short term, this might give the impression of making the web more secure fast. But in my opinion, in the long term, it is novel research that keeps pushing security forward. If novelty is not clearly protected or rewarded, I believe fewer people will be able to spend time on novel research, and the quality of that research will suffer.
